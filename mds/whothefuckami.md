## Resentment

tacSo... yeah. All that. All that to say I... had potential.

But all the potential of every person who lives or has lived, matters not if one is unable to execute on it. And something I also had, not from birth but damn fucking near, was chronic depression.

I don't know if the term _"chronic depression"_, to you, sounds bad. You might know it's a... _duller_ form of depression; one not as intense. And, if you don't think it's bad, or don't know much about it, __I **hope**, in my dead heart, that you **_never._** find. out__. Even as I mused and mulled over the thought of ending my life for fear of deep suffering, know- no, **feel**, in your heart, that I wrote this with colossal spite and resentment:

## Passions

Despite difficulties, there were moments of joy and things I appreciated in life. And, by far, the two biggest passions I had were music and creation - though I, at times, would've put fantasy in place of creation, because it is a slightly brighter passion, but it also is, in my mind, a subset contained by _'creation'_.

Of all my interests, I seemed to take a more active and meaningful role in those that involve ending up with something that didn't exist before. Drawing was the first of these, as I'm sure is often the case, but writing might arguably have been the most long-lived, with me having already taken an interest in it, albeit to a smaller degree, at a young age, and then done it up until around when my life ended, whereas drawing is something I did increasingly sporadically as I grew older.

I also always had an interest in learning some hands-on form of visual art, such as modeling or sculpting some sort of material, but never felt comfortable in exploring them. Clothing design is another such interest I indulged in over my life, some accessories and pants, but mostly punk vests and vest-adjacent outerwear; making them into physical objects wasn't quite my end of it, but I feel it still counts.

Music was even more of a constant in my life, and I'd always had a very special connection with it. As a child and teenager, singing was a seldom used but very effective way to garner approval and/or interest from those around me - I have always been impressively good at mimicking sounds and series of sounds, and that extended to singing to the point where it was very hard for me not to sing __almost exactly__ like the original singer did in the recording/version I was most used to. >>And if you are currently surprised because you have something similar, congratulations: you might be autistic as well.<<

But, beyond that, music always had a profound effect on my mood and my emotions, especially after I developed the ability to understand sung and spoken english. There are songs I could not sing because attempting to would make my voice waver and my eyes tear. There are so many songs that could, in a very short time, make me feel better, or worse; and I would use that, in both ways, just to __feel__.

tacHowever, my deep connection to music had two very specific neurological components, as well.

The quickest to mention and explain is that I had a form of synesthesia where sounds would incite what I tended to describe as shapes or textures within my head. This secondary stimulus was fairly light, though I could focus on it and feel it more clearly, and certain sounds evoked stronger secondary stimuli. Quickly changing sounds felt like animated shapes, lines or volumes, as well.

The second also relates to complexity and pattern recognition, and probably also to autism, which is that complex but still ordered music did... ___something___ very special in my brain. Genres like jazz fusion, baroque classical and progressive rock and metal would have my brain picking apart everything happening, acknowledging each thing individually, and simultaneously seeing them all connect and move and breathe together, crossing over one another, distancing over time just to merge back in over and over again... And this whole process would it light up brilliantly with satisfaction, at times to the point where I'd consider it nearly orgasmic - in the metaphorical sense, of course. And this is something I wish I had looked further into in life; I'd always wanted to be put in an fMRI machine to see just _what_ happens in my brain when it does certain things, obviously including this.

tac...And last, but definitely not least, there's... something really very special to me about seeing people being happy and having the time of their lives creating and performing music together.
